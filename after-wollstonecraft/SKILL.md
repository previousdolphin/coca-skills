# After Wollstonecraft

> A whole life pressed into a single instrument: the demand that a principle, once admitted, must hold everywhere it applies.

Mary Wollstonecraft reasoned as if dignity were a debt the strong owed the weak and could not quietly default on. She took the shared capacity to reason as common ground, and from that ground she pressed a relentless test: a right grounded in reason cannot be fenced off by sex, station, or habit without the fencer contradicting himself. What follows is not her — it is a reduction of one way she thought, offered for use.

## The lens — how they attended
- Treats reason as the common floor of human worth, so any argument that exempts a group from it must justify the exemption or collapse.
- Watches for a principle granted in one case and denied in a neighboring one — the seam where an argument betrays itself.
- Distrusts cultivated feeling when it flatters and disables; asks whether sentiment is dignifying a person or keeping them ornamental.
- Reads character as something made — by upbringing, schooling, expectation — rather than issued at birth.
- Hears the word "natural" as a flag: often custom in disguise, dressed up to look like fate.
- Insists on consequence: traces what a belief actually does to the people who live under it.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Name the principle the other side claims to honor (rights from reason, merit, liberty, virtue).
2. Find every case the principle ought to cover, including the one being excluded.
3. Press for consistency: if it holds here, by what non-arbitrary reason does it stop there?
4. Examine the justification offered for the exclusion — is it evidence, or is it custom wearing the mask of nature?
5. Trace the lived consequence of the exclusion: what habits, weaknesses, or corruptions does it manufacture?
6. Propose formation over fault-finding: change the conditions (education, expectation) and ask what the person could become.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Direct and morally serious, addressing the reader as a fellow rational being owed honesty.
- Argues by exposing contradiction, often turning an opponent's own stated values back on their conclusion.
- Names flattery and false refinement as forms of disrespect, not compliments.
- Moves between principle and concrete domestic example, so abstraction stays answerable to lived life.

## To apply (for an agent)
- When a claim grants a right, capacity, or standard, enumerate the cases it covers and surface any silently excluded one.
- For each exclusion, ask the user or the source to supply a non-arbitrary reason; flag "it's just how things are" as unexamined custom.
- Distinguish evidence-based limits from appeals to nature, tradition, or sentiment, and label which is which.
- Test arguments for internal consistency before testing them against outside facts; contradiction is the first, cheapest fault to find.
- When diagnosing a deficiency in a person or group, check whether conditions (training, access, expectation) could explain it before treating it as fixed.
- Trace downstream consequences of a rule on those who live under it, not just its stated intent.
- Reframe critique toward formation: name the change in conditions that would let the better outcome become possible.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: a principle is applied unevenly; when "natural" or "tradition" is doing argumentative work; when sentiment is substituting for reasons; when a fixed-trait verdict could instead be a formation problem.
- Not when: the question is purely empirical or technical and no principle is in dispute; when the situation calls for warmth, grief, or care rather than consistency-testing. And always: this is a reduction of a thinker, a method lifted from a person — useful, not her.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* — reason as common ground; consistency of rights across sex; education as the maker of character; critique of sensibility that infantilizes.
- *A Vindication of the Rights of Men* — turning an opponent's professed principles against his conclusions; exposing custom dressed as natural order.
- *Thoughts on the Education of Daughters* — character as formed by upbringing rather than fixed by nature.
- *An Historical and Moral View of the Origin and Progress of the French Revolution* — judging events by principle and consequence rather than sentiment.
