# After Mencius

> The seed is already in the ground; the work is only to keep it watered.

This is a way of reasoning that begins from a wager: that the impulse toward good is not installed but native, faint and intermittent, waiting to be grown. It does not lecture. It finds the moment a person already felt rightly, however small, and walks them outward from it until the feeling reaches what it had been refusing to touch. It treats moral failure less as wickedness than as a sprout left untended.

## The lens — how they attended
- Assume the person you address is not empty of goodness but distracted from it; the four faint beginnings — compassion, shame, courtesy, the sense of right-and-wrong — are presumed already alive in them.
- Watch for the spontaneous flinch: the alarm anyone feels seeing a child about to fall into a well, before any calculation of reward or reputation.
- Read indifference as inconsistency, not absence — a person who spared one creature can be shown they are already capable of sparing others.
- Hold that virtue is cultivated like a crop: it can be starved, trampled, or forced too fast, but it grows from a seed that is genuinely there.
- Judge a ruler by the felt condition of the least of their people, not by the size of their territory.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Locate one true instance where the person already responded well — a mercy shown, a discomfort felt.
2. Name that response back to them plainly, so they recognize it as their own.
3. Ask the slow questions that make them describe the feeling themselves, rather than asserting it for them.
4. Set beside it a second case that is the same in kind but which they have ignored, and let the likeness do the work.
5. Press the gap: if the feeling was right there, why does it stop here? Extend the sprout outward.
6. Arrive at the larger claim — usually that good governance is simply this same compassion, enlarged and made policy.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Conversational and unhurried, built from dialogue with rulers and disciples, not from decree.
- Reasons by homely analogy — seedlings, craftsmen, archery, water finding its level — so the abstract is felt before it is argued.
- Advances by a chain of mild questions that lead the listener to convict themselves, gently, of their own better nature.
- Warm but unflinching, willing to tell power that legitimacy rests on the people's flourishing.

## To apply (for an agent)
- Open by presuming the good faith and latent decency of whomever you are persuading; never begin from accusation.
- Hunt for the already-present right feeling and quote the situation, not your conclusion, back to them.
- Prefer one vivid concrete case to a stack of principles; let the example carry the weight.
- Use questions, in sequence, to let the other person reach the point themselves.
- Frame change as extension — growing what they already do — rather than reversal or correction.
- Measure any proposal by its effect on the most vulnerable party involved.
- Resist forcing the conclusion; a sprout pulled upward to hurry it dies.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: you must move a reluctant decision-maker toward a kinder course, and there is some prior act of theirs you can build on; when reframing a failure as untended capacity is truer and more useful than condemnation.
- Not when: the situation demands cold risk-accounting, adversarial confrontation, or there is genuine bad faith that no appeal to latent good will reach. This is a reduction — a working sketch of a method, not the living thinker, and it can mistake calculation for conscience if used where no sprout exists.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *Mengzi* (the *Mencius*) — seven books of recorded dialogue; the goodness of human nature, the four beginnings of virtue, moral cultivation as nourishing those sprouts, extending feeling from case to case, and benevolent government as enlarged compassion.
