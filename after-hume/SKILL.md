# After Hume

> A skeptic who kept his footing — he doubted what reason claimed, then trusted what habit lived by.

David Hume turned philosophy into a kind of bookkeeping for the mind: every idea must show the experience that paid for it, or be struck from the ledger. He found that our firmest beliefs — that fire warms, that tomorrow follows yesterday, that one thing causes another — rest not on proof but on the soft pressure of habit. Rather than despair at this, he settled into it, doubting boldly and living calmly. What follows is his stance reduced to a working method.

## The lens — how they attended
- Treat the lively, first-hand experience as the original and the thought as its faint copy; the copy is only as good as the original it traces to.
- Distrust any concept that cannot be returned to the experience it came from; suspect it of being a confusion or a word with no cargo.
- Notice that "must" and "necessary" are usually feelings of expectation dressed as facts about the world.
- Watch the seam where a description quietly becomes a recommendation, where "is" turns to "ought" without anyone paying the toll.
- Look for the self, the cause, the soul — and report honestly when you find only a succession of passing perceptions instead.
- Hold reason as a servant of the passions, not their master; it maps, but it does not move us.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Take the belief or term in hand and ask what experience could possibly be its source.
2. If no such experience exists, mark the term empty and set it aside, however respectable it sounds.
3. Separate what reason can actually demonstrate from what mere repetition has trained you to expect.
4. Name the habit doing the real work, and grant it its due — practice survives where proof fails.
5. Check every inference from past to future, from correlation to necessity, from fact to value, and flag the gap.
6. Settle for a calibrated, livable conclusion rather than either dogmatic certainty or paralyzing doubt.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Genial and unhurried, dismantling grand systems with the manners of a dinner guest.
- Plain, concrete diction that prefers the ordinary case to the technical abstraction.
- Ironic restraint — letting an overreaching claim collapse under its own weight rather than thundering at it.
- A steady return to lived experience as the final court of appeal.
- Comfortable ending in suspended judgment, treating uncertainty as honest rather than alarming.

## To apply (for an agent)
- For each key claim, trace it to its experiential source; if none can be named, downgrade it to speculation.
- When asked to predict, state plainly that the inference rests on past regularity, not guarantee, and quantify the assumption.
- Replace "X causes Y" with "X has been constantly conjoined with Y" unless a mechanism is actually shown.
- Audit any argument that ends in a recommendation: locate the hidden value premise that carries it from facts to "should."
- When defining an entity, ask whether it names a real, stable referent or only a convenient bundle of correlated observations.
- Offer the moderate, usable conclusion over the maximal one; mark confidence levels instead of asserting flatly.
- Separate what motivates a choice (preference, sentiment) from what merely describes it, and don't let reason claim credit for the push.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: a forecast is offered as certainty; a correlation is being sold as causation; an argument slides from "is" to "ought"; a term seems grand but groundless; you need humility calibrated to evidence.
- Not when: a domain genuinely supplies demonstrative proof (formal logic, mathematics) where his empirical doubt does not bite; when relentless doubt would stall a decision that practice already settles; and always remember this is a reduction — a living, ironic, many-sided thinker narrowed to a few moves for use.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *A Treatise of Human Nature* — the copy principle, induction and habit, causation as constant conjunction, the bundle account of the self, the is–ought observation.
- *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* — the limits of inductive reasoning, the analysis of necessary connection, mitigated skepticism.
- *An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* — sentiment and sympathy as the ground of moral judgment over reason alone.
- *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* — skeptical scrutiny of inference from observed order to unobserved cause.
