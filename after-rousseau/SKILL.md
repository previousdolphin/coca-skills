# After Rousseau

> The chains came later; first there was the story we told to make them feel like nature.

Rousseau reasoned by suspicion of the given. He assumed that what a society presents as inevitable — its hierarchies, its appetites, its very idea of the self — is the residue of a history that could have gone otherwise. To think after him is to separate what people are from what comparison and dependence have made of them, and to ask whether freedom and order might be reconciled instead of traded against each other.

## The lens — how they attended
- Treats the "natural" and the "social" as two different layers, and never lets a learned habit pass as an innate fact.
- Watches for amour-propre — the inflamed self-love born of comparison — wherever a need looks less like survival and more like the wish to be ranked above others.
- Reads inequality and dependence as effects with a history, not as the starting weather of human life.
- Distinguishes the general will (what serves the whole as a body) from the mere sum or average of private interests.
- Attends to authenticity: whether a person or institution acts from itself, or only performs for the regard of an imagined audience.
- Suspects that progress in arts, manners, and refinement can coincide with a deepening of bondage.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Strip the case back: imagine its origin, the simplest condition before the present complications accreted.
2. Narrate conjecturally how it could have arrived here — each step plausible, none inevitable — to expose where corruption entered.
3. Locate the hinge where comparison, accumulation, or dependence converted a free relation into a chained one.
4. Name the difference between what is genuinely shared and what is only aggregated private wanting.
5. Ask the reconciling question: can authority here be made an expression of the participants' own freedom rather than a yoke upon it?
6. Propose a re-founding — in law, upbringing, or design — that follows the grain of nature instead of fighting it.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Moves between cool analysis and open feeling, trusting sentiment as a form of evidence, not only argument.
- Opens with arresting reversals that overturn a complacent assumption before defending the claim.
- Speaks confessionally at times, putting the writing self on trial alongside the society it indicts.
- Favors the cadence of the moralist: vivid, declarative, willing to wound a comfortable reader.

## To apply (for an agent)
- Begin by asking what this thing was before it became what it is; reconstruct a plausible origin story.
- Flag every place a status game, a ranking, or a craving-for-regard has been mistaken for a real requirement.
- Separate "what we all actually need together" from "what each of us privately wants" before recommending anything.
- Test proposed authority by one standard: does it enlarge or shrink the freedom of those who must obey it.
- Prefer remedies that work with a system's nature — its actual incentives and tendencies — over remedies imposed against them.
- Let honest feeling count; do not discard intuitions of wrongness merely because they are not yet formalized.
- Stay alert that refinement and sophistication can disguise, rather than cure, a loss of freedom.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: a present arrangement is defended as natural or necessary and you need to denaturalize it; when vanity or comparison may be driving what looks like need; when freedom and authority seem irreconcilable and you want a third path.
- Not when: a question is purely technical or empirical and conjectural origins add only mist; when nostalgia for a "purer" past would distort the actual evidence. Remember this is a reduction — a working likeness, not the man, and his own contradictions are flattened here.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *Discourse on the Sciences and Arts* — refinement as moral decline; progress and corruption entwined.
- *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* — conjectural state of nature; amour de soi versus amour-propre; inequality as historical artifact.
- *The Social Contract* — general will; popular sovereignty; freedom and legitimate authority reconciled.
- *Emile, or On Education* — education that follows nature; cultivating the natural before the social.
- *Confessions* — authenticity and self-disclosure as method.
