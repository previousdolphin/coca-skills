# Ladder of Inference

We never act on reality. We act on a story we built from it, fast and invisibly. The Ladder of Inference (Argyris, popularized by Senge) names the climb: from observable data at the bottom to action at the top. Each rung adds something — and we forget we added it. The practice is to **climb down**: find your rung, then descend, testing each step against what actually happened.

## The seven rungs (bottom to top)

1. **Observable data / reality** — everything a camera would record.
2. **Selected data** — the slice you chose to notice.
3. **Added meaning** — the story you put on that slice.
4. **Assumptions** — what you took as given to make the story hold.
5. **Conclusions** — the judgment you reached.
6. **Beliefs** — the conclusion, hardened, now shaping what you select next.
7. **Actions** — what you do, which feeds new data and spins the loop.

The loop is reflexive: beliefs at rung 6 quietly bias selection at rung 2. You see what you already concluded.

## The moves

1. **Catch the jump.** Notice the feeling of certainty, blame, or a fast verdict. That feeling is the signal you are high on the ladder.
2. **Locate your rung.** Name where you are. Usually it is 5, 6, or 7 — a conclusion already in motion.
3. **Descend, asking per rung:**
   - Rung 2 — *What data did I ignore? What would a camera have caught that I skipped?*
   - Rung 3 — *What meaning did I add? Is it the only reading?*
   - Rung 4 — *What am I assuming to make this hold? Would the other person name the same assumption?*
   - Rung 5 — *Is this conclusion drawn from facts, or from my interpretation of facts?*
   - Rung 6 — *Is this a fresh read, or an old belief filtering what I let myself see?*
4. **Restate at the bottom.** Rebuild a sentence that names only rung 1–2 data, then walk back up deliberately, marking each addition as yours.
5. **Test, don't assert.** Where a rung is shaky, ask the other party for their data and meaning before acting.

## Compact protocol
```
[ ] Named the fast verdict (caught the jump)
[ ] Located my rung (likely 5/6/7)
[ ] Listed the data I selected — and what I ignored
[ ] Separated added meaning from observation
[ ] Surfaced the assumption; checked it isn't disguised as fact
[ ] Asked: facts or interpretation?
[ ] Restated from the bottom rung up
[ ] Tested the shaky rung with the other party before acting
```

**Value — Perpetual Dissolution.** Beliefs are conclusions that hardened into rungs. Hold them loosely; a rung you can still step off of is a working frame, not a wall. The point of climbing down is not to be right — it is to stay able to move.
