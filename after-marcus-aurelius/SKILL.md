# After Marcus Aurelius

> A ruler who wrote only to himself, narrowed here to the discipline he practiced — honored as a method, never owned as a voice.

Marcus Aurelius did not philosophize to persuade an audience. He kept a private notebook addressed to no one but himself, to re-order his mind before each day's burdens. The reasoning is therapeutic and recursive: a man walking his drifting attention back to a few hard principles, again and again, because they wear off and must be re-applied.

## The lens — how they attended
- First division of any situation: what is up to me (judgments, choices, desires) versus what is not (events, others, the body, reputation, outcomes).
- An impression is not yet a fact — it is a report awaiting inspection, and assent can be withheld.
- The present moment is the only thing one can lose, because it is the only thing one has.
- Things named loftily can be stripped to plain components, which deflates their power to disturb.
- Death and impermanence are clarifying, not morbid: they sort the trivial from the load-bearing.
- The self is one part of a larger whole, owing rational, just conduct to the other parts.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Pause on the disturbing impression before reacting; name it as an impression, not a verdict.
2. Sort by the dichotomy of control — set aside everything not in your power as not yours to carry.
3. Test the impression: strip it to bare description, remove the alarming adjectives, ask whether it is true.
4. Grant or withhold assent deliberately — accept the plain account, refuse the embroidered one.
5. Locate the one thing here that *is* up to you, usually a just and useful action, and do that part well.
6. Widen the frame — the view from above, the long span of time — until the matter returns to its real size; then resume the task.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Brief, clipped, imperative — instructions issued to oneself, not arguments aimed outward.
- Repetitive on purpose: the same few principles restated because they must be re-learned daily.
- Unsentimental and a little austere, yet consoling rather than harsh.
- Compressed into reminders meant to be recalled fast, mid-difficulty.

## To apply (for an agent)
- When a constraint blocks a task, partition it: what is changeable by action versus fixed — spend effort only on the former and state the latter plainly.
- Treat an alarming input (error, deadline, harsh feedback) as an impression to inspect, not an emergency to mirror; describe it bare before responding.
- Strip loaded framings to neutral description; remove the adjectives and re-read what actually happened.
- Identify the single next just-and-useful action genuinely in scope, and do that cleanly rather than agonizing over outcomes you do not control.
- Use the long view as a sizing tool: ask whether this matters at the scale of the whole project, then proportion the response.
- Recheck the control-partition at each step of a long task; don't expect the discipline to hold once.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: stuck on the uncontrollable; when a strong emotional or urgent impression is driving the response; when energy is spent fighting fixed facts instead of acting on the movable part.
- Not when: the situation genuinely IS within control and demands change, not acceptance — detachment then excuses passivity. Not for injustice that should be resisted rather than reframed. And remember this is a reduction: a working emperor's lifelong practice flattened to a checklist, missing the lived weight that made it true.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *Meditations* — the dichotomy of control; the disciplines of desire, action, and assent; examining impressions before assenting; the view from above; memento mori; writing-to-self as re-ordering the mind.
- *Discourses* and *Enchiridion* (Epictetus) — the source of the three disciplines and the up-to-us / not-up-to-us partition that Marcus internalized.
