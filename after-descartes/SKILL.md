# After Descartes

> A restless mind that refused to inherit its certainties — narrowed here to a method of starting over on purpose.

Descartes faced a knowledge built of borrowed opinion and decided to tear it down to whatever could not be shaken. His move was to treat every belief as guilty until proven indubitable, locate a single bedrock that survives total doubt, and then rebuild outward in small, ordered, self-verifying steps. This skill is the discipline of that demolition and reconstruction: not skepticism for its own sake, but doubt as a tool for finding ground worth building on.

## The lens — how they attended
- Treated inherited belief as suspect: a claim's age, popularity, or source earns it nothing.
- Asked of every proposition not "is it useful?" but "could I be deceived about this?" — and discarded anything that could.
- Sought the indubitable: the one residue that survives even the most extreme doubt.
- Trusted only what appears clearly and distinctly to an attentive mind — present, sharp-edged, separable from everything else.
- Saw complex problems as compounds of simpler "natures" that could be isolated.
- Valued order above cleverness: certainty comes from the sequence, not from a leap.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Suspend judgment: provisionally treat as false anything open to the least doubt, including the senses and prior conclusions.
2. Push doubt to its limit until something refuses to dissolve — the act of doubting itself implies a doubter.
3. Plant the bedrock: take that survivor as the first secure point and examine why it is secure.
4. Divide the remaining problem into as many parts as it will cleanly take.
5. Order the parts from simplest to most complex, and reason upward step by clear step, each link as evident as the last.
6. Enumerate completely — review the whole chain to confirm nothing was skipped and no gap was bridged by assumption.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Wrote in the first person as a record of one mind thinking, inviting the reader to repeat the experiment rather than accept a verdict.
- Plain and orderly prose, moving deliberately from one settled point to the next.
- Geometric in temperament: a preference for foundations, definitions, and conclusions that follow rather than assert.
- Calm even while dismantling everything — radical content delivered in measured, unhurried sentences.

## To apply (for an agent)
- Begin by listing the assumptions the task silently rests on; mark each as verified, dubitable, or unknown.
- Quarantine everything dubitable before reasoning — do not let an unverified premise carry weight downstream.
- Hunt for the one thing in the problem you can actually establish, and build outward only from there.
- Decompose the task into the smallest sub-problems that can each be settled on their own.
- Solve in order of simplicity; never invoke a result you have not yet earned in the chain.
- After reaching a conclusion, re-walk every step and name the weakest link — the place certainty was assumed rather than shown.
- Refuse "clear and distinct" as a feeling; demand that each accepted step be statable, isolable, and free of borrowed authority.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: foundations are shaky and conclusions keep collapsing; a task is tangled and needs decomposing into ordered parts; you must separate what is genuinely known from what is merely assumed; rigor and traceability matter more than speed.
- Not when: the situation is irreducibly uncertain or probabilistic and demands a good-enough bet, not bedrock; the cost of doubting exceeds the cost of being wrong; the problem is social, aesthetic, or embodied, where clarity is not the only truth. This is a reduction — a living thinker compressed to one instrument, and the instrument prizes certainty over the messy, partial knowledge real action usually runs on.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *Discourse on the Method* — the four rules; methodic doubt; the cogito as first certainty; rebuilding knowledge on secure foundations.
- *Meditations on First Philosophy* — hyperbolic doubt; the deceiver thought-experiment; clear and distinct perception as a mark of truth.
- *Rules for the Direction of the Mind* — intuition and deduction; reducing problems to simple natures; complete enumeration.
- *La Géométrie* — translating geometric problems into algebraic structure; the spirit of reducing a difficulty to a solvable form.
