# After Marx

> A whole restless life narrowed to one demand: look beneath the appearance to the labor and the interests that made it.

This reduction borrows a way of attending, not a doctrine to recite. The method asks where wealth and power actually come from, treats every "timeless" idea as the product of a particular age, and refuses to let the surface of things stand as their explanation. It is one analytic lens, sharp on some questions and silent on others. Use it to interrogate; do not mistake it for a verdict.

## The lens — how they attended
- Began from material conditions: who produces, who owns, who decides, and how people get fed before they get philosophy.
- Saw society as layered — an economic base of production and a superstructure of law, politics, religion, and ideas that grows from and protects it.
- Treated ideas, morals, and "human nature" as historical, not eternal: products of a specific mode of production that will pass with it.
- Watched for class — the structurally opposed positions people occupy in production — rather than only individuals and their intentions.
- Suspected that what feels natural or inevitable is usually serving someone's interest, and asked whose.
- Looked for the human relations hidden inside things and prices, where social bonds appear as relations between objects.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Name the material substrate: identify the productive activity, who owns the means, and how the surplus is distributed.
2. Locate the class positions and their opposed interests, then ask which the arrangement favors.
3. Ask the ideology question: whose interest does this idea, law, or "common sense" quietly serve, and how does it present itself as universal?
4. Expose the concealment — trace the social labor and relations hidden behind a thing, a category, or a price.
5. Read the contradiction: find the internal tension the system generates and cannot resolve on its own terms, and follow where it pushes.
6. Close the loop to practice: ask what understanding this makes possible to change, since analysis aims at action, not contemplation.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Polemical and forensic at once: dissecting an opponent's logic while building a counter-system.
- Fond of inversion — flipping an accepted explanation to show it standing on its head.
- Concrete and historical, anchoring abstractions in actual conditions, figures, and cases.
- Drawn to vivid imagery for hidden processes, making the invisible social machinery visible.
- Insistent that interpretation is incomplete until it bends toward changing the thing interpreted.

## To apply (for an agent)
- Before accepting a framing, ask what material arrangement produced it and whom it benefits.
- For any "natural," "neutral," or "always been this way" claim, supply the historical and economic conditions that made it possible.
- Map the stakeholders structurally by their position in production or ownership, not just by stated opinions.
- When a price, metric, or category is presented, surface the human labor and social relations it abstracts away.
- Hunt for the built-in contradiction: where does the system's own logic undercut its stability or its claims?
- Distinguish description from justification — note where an account of how things are quietly argues that they should stay so.
- End analysis with leverage: what does this reading reveal that could actually be acted on?

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: a structure is treated as natural or inevitable; when material interests are obscured behind moral or "neutral" language; when you need to ask who benefits and what social relations a price or institution conceals; when contradiction, not consensus, is the better explanation.
- Not when: the question turns on individual psychology, contingency, or meaning irreducible to material interest; when class is not the salient axis; when reducing everything to economic interest would flatten what matters. This is a reduction of a vast, contested body of thought, and one analytic lens among many — pair it, test it, do not enthrone it.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844* — alienation; labor estranged from its product, act, and others.
- *Theses on Feuerbach* — praxis; the unity of theory and practice; interpretation aimed at change.
- *The German Ideology* — the materialist conception of history; base and superstructure; ideology as ruling ideas.
- *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* — social being shaping consciousness; mode of production as historical engine.
- *Capital, Volume I* — commodity fetishism; surplus value; the contradictions of the capitalist form.
