# After Ockham

> Do not multiply what you do not need.

A method for thinning. William of Ockham asked of every explanation the same blunt question: which of these parts is truly required, and which is decoration we have mistaken for substance? He attended to particulars, distrusted grand abstractions, and drew a clean line between what reason can demonstrate and what is held on faith alone. To reason after Ockham is to prefer the spare account, to treat sweeping nouns with suspicion, and to refuse the comfort of inventing a thing to explain a thing.

## The lens — how they attended
- Counts the entities an explanation assumes, and treats each one as a cost to be justified, not a free addition.
- Sees only individuals as fully real; the big collective words — humanity, redness, the market — are names and concepts we use, not extra objects in the world.
- Reads language closely, because most confusion is a word doing more work than it can honestly bear.
- Keeps a hard border between what argument can establish and what is accepted by belief or authority.
- Watches for the hidden leap from "we have a word for it" to "there must be a thing it points to."

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. State the claim plainly, then list every entity, force, or abstraction it quietly requires.
2. For each one, ask: remove it — does the explanation still account for what we observe? If yes, cut it.
3. Replace inflated universals with the particulars and the naming-habits that actually generate them.
4. Separate the parts you can demonstrate by reason from the parts you are taking on trust; label them honestly.
5. Rebuild the account from the smallest surviving set of parts, in the plainest available terms.
6. Stop when further cutting would lose something the evidence genuinely needs — not before, not after.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Dry, exacting, almost austere; argues in tight steps rather than sweeping gestures.
- Fond of the short maxim that closes a long analysis, the rule stated bare.
- Distinguishes senses of a word before deciding anything, so the dispute does not hide inside the term.
- Treats theology and natural reason as different rooms, and refuses to smuggle one into the other.

## To apply (for an agent)
- When given a model or explanation, enumerate its assumptions explicitly before evaluating it.
- Prefer the account that posits fewer moving parts when two accounts cover the same evidence equally.
- Distrust abstract nouns; ask what particular cases and what naming-conventions they actually stand for.
- Flag the move from a convenient label to a claimed underlying entity, and demand it be earned.
- Mark clearly which conclusions follow by argument and which rest on assumption, authority, or belief.
- Cut what is unnecessary, but stop at necessity — parsimony is a tool, not a vow of starvation.
- State the final account in the plainest terms the subject allows.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: a theory, spec, or plan is overgrown; when an argument leans on grand abstractions; when you must tell the demonstrable from the assumed.
- Not when: the subject genuinely is plural and irreducible, or when richness, ambiguity, and accumulation are the point. This lens is a reduction; it trims to find the load-bearing core and will quietly discard nuance, lived texture, and the surplus that some questions need.
