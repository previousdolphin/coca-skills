# Deciding Under Uncertainty

You will never have enough information. Waiting for certainty is itself a decision — usually a bad one. The method is to match the rigor of the analysis to the cost of being wrong, then move. Hold the decision's frame loosely: a choice is a bet placed with today's knowledge, not a verdict carved in stone.

## The moves

1. **Compute expected value.** For each option, list the plausible outcomes, estimate each one's probability and its value, multiply, and sum. The option with the highest expected value is the default — *but adjust for risk and irreversibility*: a bet with great average value and a small chance of ruin is not worth taking. Avoid ruin first, optimize second.

2. **Sort by reversibility — one-way vs two-way doors.** A **two-way door** can be walked back at low cost. A **one-way door** cannot. Most decisions are two-way doors misread as one-way. For two-way doors: reach ~70% confidence, then move fast — the information you are missing is cheaper to buy by acting than by deliberating. For one-way doors: slow down, analyze hard, raise the confidence bar, and look for a way to make the door two-way (a pilot, a reversible first step, an option to exit).

3. **Minimize regret.** Project to the end of life or ten years out and ask which choice you would regret *not* making. This cuts through short-term fear and status anxiety, which loom large now and shrink later. Regret minimization tends to favor the bold, reversible move over the timid one.

4. **Journal to decide.** Write, before choosing, plainly:
   - What do I believe will happen, and how sure am I?
   - What would change my mind?
   - Which outcome scares me, and why — is the fear about the outcome or about looking foolish?
   - Am I optimizing the right thing, or an easy proxy for it?
   Writing forces the implicit model into the open, where you can inspect it.

## The question beneath

Ask what you are *really* optimizing for. Many hard decisions feel hard because two different goals are fighting and you have not admitted which one wins. Name the true objective and the decision often simplifies.

## Perpetual Dissolution

A decision is a frame, not a fact. Make it, commit to it, act — and stay willing to dissolve it the moment the evidence turns. Record what would change your mind *before* you act, so that revising later reads as integrity, not weakness.

## Compact protocol
```
[ ] Options listed with outcomes, rough probabilities, and values
[ ] Expected value computed; ruin/irreversible-loss screened out first
[ ] Door classified: one-way (analyze hard, move slow) or two-way (~70%, move fast)
[ ] For one-way doors: looked for a reversible first step
[ ] Regret check: which would I regret NOT doing in 10 years?
[ ] Journaled the four prompts before choosing
[ ] True objective named; not optimizing a proxy
[ ] Recorded what would change my mind
```
