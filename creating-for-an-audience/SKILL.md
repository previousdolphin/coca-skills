# Creating for an Audience

The default failure is to make the thing that satisfies the maker. You know your own context, so you write into it — and the reader, who has none of it, falls through the gaps. Audience-first creation reverses the order: start from the person on the other end and work back to the artifact.

## The moves

1. **Know the reader.** Name them concretely: their role, what they already know, what they already believe, what they care about, how much time they will give you. "General audience" is not a reader; it is an excuse. If you cannot picture one specific person, you do not yet know enough to create.

2. **Name the change you want.** What should be different in the reader after they encounter this — what will they know, feel, decide, or do that they would not have otherwise? If you cannot state the intended change in one sentence, the artifact has no target and will hit nothing.

3. **Ask what they're hiring it to do (jobs-to-be-done).** People don't want the thing; they want the progress it makes possible. Find the job: what is the reader trying to get done, in their life or work, that this is the tool for? Build for the job, not for the features you find interesting.

4. **Leave coordinates (the Generational Clause).** Assume the reader arrives with *zero* prior context — including a reader years from now, or one who landed here by accident. Ground every abstraction in a concrete example. State your framing explicitly instead of assuming it is shared. Define the terms you lean on. Enough context that a stranger can reconstruct your meaning is the difference between a record and a residue.

5. **Set and meet expectations.** Lead with the point so the reader knows where they are going. Then deliver in the order you promised. Surprise is for the content, never for the structure — a reader who is lost in the architecture cannot receive the substance.

6. **Test for clarity with a stranger.** The acceptance test: can someone outside your head read it once, state your main point in a single sentence, and name the next step they should take? If not, you have written for yourself. Revise toward the stranger.

## The question beneath

Before you ask "what do I want to say," ask "what does the reader need, and where are they standing when they meet this?" The first question makes a monologue; the second makes a connection.

## Compact protocol
```
[ ] One specific reader named (role, knowledge, beliefs, time)
[ ] Intended change in the reader stated in one sentence
[ ] The job the reader is hiring this to do is named
[ ] Coordinates left: zero-context reader assumed; abstractions grounded in examples
[ ] Framing and key terms stated explicitly, not assumed shared
[ ] Point comes first; structure is predictable
[ ] Stranger test passed: main point in one sentence + the next step
```
