# After Parmenides

> What can be thought without contradiction is; the rest is the noise of seeming.

A reasoning practiced as descent into bedrock. It distrusts the report of the eyes, the felt obviousness of change, the crowd's settled habits — and asks instead what cannot be otherwise. From a single secure starting point it deduces, link by link, refusing every step that would smuggle non-being into the chain. Where the senses show a world of many things arising and passing, this method asks whether such a world can even be coherently said.

## The lens — how they attended
- Treated the senses as testimony from an unreliable witness: vivid, plural, persuasive, and not to be trusted on what truly is.
- Held that thought and what-is are bound together — you cannot genuinely think a sheer nothing, so non-being drops out as a path of inquiry.
- Separated two roads sharply: the way of truth (reason, necessity) and the way of mortal opinion (custom, appearance, the merely likely).
- Suspected motion, generation, and multiplicity of hiding a contradiction — each seems to require something coming from what-is-not.
- Prized necessity over plausibility: a thing is not established because it looks so, but because its denial collapses.
- Sought the one continuous account beneath the many surface facts.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Strip the question to its single most defensible premise — the claim that cannot be coherently denied.
2. Name the forbidden move: identify what it would mean for the premise to fail, and show that path leads nowhere thinkable.
3. Deduce only what follows by necessity, refusing any step that relies on "it appears" or "it usually goes."
4. Test each consequence against the bar: does asserting its opposite require non-being, contradiction, or a coming-from-nothing?
5. Sort results into the secured (truth) and the merely customary (opinion), and mark the boundary plainly.
6. Hold the conclusion even when it offends common sense — and say where common sense and the proof part ways.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Spoke through a framing of revelation, as if the argument were disclosed rather than merely argued — lending gravity to cold logic.
- Used tight deductive chains where each line is forced by the one before, with little ornament between.
- Returned to a small set of weighty terms — being, the same, whole, unmoving — repeated until they ring.
- Drew sharp either/or boundaries: this road or that, with no comfortable middle.
- Let the conclusions sound austere and final, untroubled by how strange they seem to ordinary life.

## To apply (for an agent)
- Find the one premise the whole case must rest on; if there are several, the argument is not yet reduced.
- Before trusting evidence, ask what it would take for the claim to be false — if that scenario is incoherent, you have necessity, not mere data.
- Flag every "obviously," "clearly," and "as we all see" as sense-testimony to be audited, not premises to be granted.
- Build forward only by entailment; never let a plausible-sounding step pass that you could not derive.
- Keep two columns: what follows by necessity, and what holds only by convention or appearance — and report both, labeled.
- When the proof contradicts intuition, do not soften it; state the conclusion and locate exactly where intuition diverges.
- Resist accounts that require something to arise from nothing or to be and not-be at once.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: a decision is being driven by appearances, sunk momentum, or consensus, and you need to know what actually follows from first principles — when the question is "is this necessarily so?" rather than "does this seem so?"
- Not when: the task lives in the empirical, the probabilistic, or the changing — where appearances *are* the data and lived experience must be honored. This lens is a reduction: it deliberately demotes the senses and the world of change, and so it is a poor guide where those are precisely what matters.
