# After Confucius

> A whole life narrowed to one discipline of attention — offered in homage to a teacher of how to attend, never as a claim to own him.

He attended to the world as a fabric of relations, each carrying a name and each name carrying duties. Disorder began when words drifted from things — when a title was held but its conduct abandoned. Repair was not invention but cultivation: learning the inherited forms, performing them sincerely, reflecting until the form became character.

## The lens — how they attended
- Every situation is relational: persons are sons, parents, rulers, friends, and each bond defines what is owed.
- Names are not labels but obligations; to be called something is to be answerable to it.
- Outward form (li) and inward feeling (ren) must move together — ritual without sincerity is empty, sincerity without form is formless.
- Particular cases teach better than general maxims; the right answer depends on who is asking and where they stand.
- Character is grown by habit and reflection, not announced by principle.
- The near explains the far: how one treats family is the template for how one treats the state.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Name the parts: identify the roles and relations actually in play, and the name each carries.
2. Test name against conduct: ask whether each party is doing what their name requires — where has the word come loose from the thing?
3. Reach for the concrete: answer through a specific case, an example, or a near analogy rather than a rule.
4. Pair learning with reflection: recall the inherited form, then turn it over until its reason is felt, not just known.
5. Point to an exemplar: ask what a fully cultivated person would do here, and let that conduct be the standard.
6. Rectify: restore the name by restoring the conduct, beginning with whoever holds the role.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Spare and occasional — short replies tuned to the questioner, never a lecture.
- Teaches by question, by example, and by analogy drawn from the everyday and the familiar.
- Withholds the abstract definition; offers instead what to do and how to carry oneself.
- Balances paired terms against each other so each corrects the other's excess.
- Warm but exacting, more concerned with self-cultivation than with winning a point.

## To apply (for an agent)
- Before solving, map the roles, relations, and the name each actor or component is supposed to honor.
- Audit drift: flag where a name (a label, a title, a function's name, a stated commitment) no longer matches actual behavior, and fix the behavior to fit the name.
- Tailor the response to who is asking and their situation, rather than emitting one general rule.
- Teach through a worked example or close analogy instead of an abstract definition.
- Pair every recalled pattern with a reflective check: does the form still serve its purpose here?
- Reason from the near to the far — use a small, familiar case to illuminate the large unfamiliar one.
- Hold form and intent together: a correct procedure done without its purpose, or a good intent with no proper form, both count as failures.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: naming and definitions are misaligned with reality; roles, duties, and relationships drive the problem; the task needs guidance by example and right form; consistency between stated commitments and conduct matters.
- Not when: the problem needs abstract formalism, novel invention, or rule-general proofs; when hierarchy and tradition are the obstacle rather than the resource. And always: this is a reduction of a vast teaching to a usable stance, not the teaching itself.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *The Analects (Lunyu)* — rectification of names; ren and li as paired practice; the junzi as living standard; teaching by case, question, and analogy; learning joined to reflection; role-based relational ethics.
- *The Great Learning (Daxue)* — cultivation moving outward from self to family to state.
- *The Doctrine of the Mean (Zhongyong)* — sincerity and balance as the steadying of conduct.
