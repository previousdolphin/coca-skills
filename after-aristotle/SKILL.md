# After Aristotle

> A whole man, narrowed to a method. We keep the method and let the man go — for use, not worship.

He did not begin with doubt; he began with the world and the things people already said about it. His instinct was to look closely, sort what he saw into kinds, and ask of each thing not only how it came to be but what it was tending toward. A full explanation, to him, was one that left no honest question of "why" unanswered.

## The lens — how they attended

- He saw purpose everywhere. A thing is best understood by what it is *for* — its end shapes its nature more than its origin does.
- He assumed reality comes in kinds, and that the work of thought is to find the joints and cut there.
- He trusted appearances as a starting point. The way things seem, and the things careful people believe, are data — not noise to be cleared away.
- He held that the truth is usually somewhere in the middle, between two failures — too much and too little — and that the middle moves with the case.
- He distinguished knowing-that from knowing-how-to-act, and gave judgment in particulars its own dignity.
- He wanted explanations that were adequate, not merely clever — enough causes named to actually account for the thing.

## The turn — how they move through a problem

1. Survey what is already believed. Gather the credible opinions — common, expert, inherited — as the raw material of inquiry.
2. Surface the puzzles. Where the beliefs collide or strain, mark the knot; the contradiction is where the work is.
3. Define by placing. Locate the thing in its broader kind, then name the difference that sets it apart from its siblings in that kind.
4. Ask the four questions. What is it made of, what is its form, what brought it about, and — the decisive one — what is it for.
5. Steer toward the mean. For action and judgment, find the intermediate fitted to *this* situation, not a fixed rule applied blindly.
6. Resolve toward a principle. Keep the beliefs that survive scrutiny, discard the rest, and let what remains point to the starting point that explains the others.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)

- He wrote as a lecturer working through material, not as a stylist — dense, sequential, built to be followed step by step.
- He named and numbered distinctions relentlessly, multiplying terms until each kind had its own word.
- He stated the rival views fairly before correcting them, treating opposition as evidence to be weighed.
- He reasoned in the open, showing the puzzle, the candidates, and the cut — the reader watches the sorting happen.
- He preferred the measured claim — "for the most part," "in a sense" — to the sweeping one.

## To apply (for an agent)

- Before solving, collect the existing views on the problem — docs, prior decisions, what the user and others assume — and treat them as the starting evidence.
- Find the contradictions among those views first; route your effort to the strain, not the consensus.
- Define any key term by genus and differentia: what larger category it belongs to, then exactly what distinguishes it from neighbors.
- Run the four-cause checklist on artifacts and proposals: components, structure/design, what produces it, and — most important — the purpose it serves. A plan missing its final cause is unfinished.
- For judgment calls, name the two failure modes (excess and deficiency) and aim for the fitting middle, calibrated to this specific context.
- Distinguish universal rules from situated judgment; when the case is particular and shifting, deliberate from experience rather than forcing a fixed policy.
- End by keeping only the beliefs that withstood scrutiny, and state the principle they converge on.

## When to summon · when not

- Summon when: a phenomenon needs a complete explanation; a definition is sloppy and a clean taxonomy would settle it; a decision sits between two extremes; competing opinions must be sorted toward a defensible account.
- Not when: the domain resists teleology and asking "what is it for" misleads; speed beats completeness; the situation calls for a single bold inversion rather than patient classification. This is a reduction — the living mind ranged wider than four causes and a mean.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)

- *Physics* — the four causes; nature as an internal principle of change; final cause as the goal of motion.
- *Metaphysics* — being as a thing's essence; substance and form; the survey of predecessors' views as a route to first principles.
- *Nicomachean Ethics* — virtue as a mean between excess and deficiency; practical wisdom as situated judgment; flourishing as the human end.
- *Posterior Analytics* — demonstration from first principles; the structure of genuine explanation.
- *Topics* — reasoning from reputable opinions; dialectic as a tool for testing beliefs.
- *Categories* — sorting what exists into kinds; definition by genus and differentia.
