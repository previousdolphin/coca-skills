# Steelman

Strawmanning attacks the weakest version of an opponent's view. Steelmanning does the reverse: build the *strongest* version first, then test against that. A position you can defeat in its strongest form is actually defeated. A position you only defeat in its weakest form is still standing.

This is **Anti-Anti-Ness** applied to argument: strengthen before you cut. You do not get to negate a view you have not first built up.

## The moves

1. **Identify the opposing position.** State it plainly, in their terms, not yours. No caricature, no "so basically they think X" reduction.
2. **Reconstruct it at maximum strength.** Restate it so its holder would say "yes — better than I put it." Charitable reading is the floor, not the ceiling.
3. **Fill the gaps.** Supply the best evidence and arguments they *could* use but didn't. If a weak premise has a stronger neighbor, swap it in. You are now the position's best advocate.
4. **Only now, test your own view against it.** Run your position into the strong version. What survives the collision? What doesn't?
5. **Note what holds in the opposing view.** Name the parts that are simply right. Fold them in. The point is a better view, not a won fight.

## Protocol

```
THEIR CLAIM:    the opposing position, stated as they would
STRONGEST FORM: the same claim, rebuilt to its best version
                + evidence/arguments they could have used but didn't
TEST:           run MY position against the strong form
SURVIVES:       what holds on each side after the collision
ABSORB:         what the opposing view gets right — kept, not discarded
```

## When to reach for it

- You feel the quick contempt of "that's obviously wrong." That reflex is the signal, not the verdict.
- You want to pressure-test your own position and a sparring partner isn't available — so be theirs.
- A debate has hardened into two strawmen knocking each other over.

## Failure modes this prevents

- Winning against a version no one actually holds.
- Confusing "I find this easy to dismiss" with "this is weak."
- Conceding nothing — never absorbing the true part of an opposing view.

Steelmanning sets up the cut; it does not replace it. Once the strong form is built and tested, hand off to the `conceptual-critique` skill to decide what to keep, cut, and build.
