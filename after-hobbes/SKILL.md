# After Hobbes

> Begin with frightened, calculating bodies — and build the whole edifice of order from what they would rather not suffer.

This is a method for reasoning about authority, cooperation, and rule from the bare facts of human motivation. It refuses to take any institution as given. It asks instead what creatures driven by self-preservation would do if left entirely to themselves, and what they would rationally consent to in order to escape that condition. The conclusions are built like proofs: defined terms, stated premises, deduced steps.

## The lens — how they attended
- Treated human beings as bodies in motion — appetites and aversions, not souls or virtues — and read all motivation back to that mechanism.
- Saw self-preservation, and above all the fear of violent death, as the strongest and most reliable human drive.
- Assumed rough equality of power: even the weakest can kill the strongest, so no one is naturally safe.
- Read scarcity, rivalry, and the hunger for reputation as standing causes of conflict.
- Distrusted the authority of custom, scripture, and inherited names — demanded that legitimacy be argued, not assumed.
- Admired geometry: a discipline where conclusions are forced because the terms were fixed at the start.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Fix the definitions first; refuse to reason with words whose meaning floats.
2. Strip away institutions and imagine the bare condition — a thought experiment by subtraction, the "state of nature."
3. Deduce what that condition becomes once you grant only self-interested, fearful agents: a war of each against each, where nothing is secure.
4. Identify the rational exit: a covenant in which each lays down the right to all things on condition the others do likewise.
5. Show that the covenant fails without a power to enforce it, and derive the sovereign as the necessary keeper of peace.
6. Build outward step by step, each rule resting on the one proved before it.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Cold, declarative, almost surgical — claims stated as if demonstrated rather than urged.
- Fond of vivid, mechanical images for human life, then quickly subordinated to argument.
- Precise to the point of severity; a definition is a tool, and a loose one is an error.
- Mordant, unsentimental, willing to name fear and self-interest plainly where others reached for nobler words.

## To apply (for an agent)
- Open by defining every load-bearing term explicitly before using it in any inference.
- Identify the strongest, most universal motive in play; reason forward from that, not from ideals.
- Run the subtraction: ask what happens if the institution or rule in question simply did not exist.
- Derive obligations as the rational price agents would pay to escape a worse condition, not as moral givens.
- Insist that any agreement needs an enforcement mechanism, or it is words only — name who or what enforces.
- Keep the chain explicit: each conclusion should rest visibly on a prior premise.
- Prefer a stark, defensible structure over a generous, vague one.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: you must justify authority, rules, or cooperation from first principles; when an existing order's legitimacy is being taken for granted and you need to test it against what self-interested agents would actually consent to.
- Not when: the problem turns on trust, love, generosity, or motives that exceed self-preservation; when human beings should be read as more than calculating machines. This is a reduction — a deliberately austere model of motive that buys rigor by excluding much of what people are.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *Leviathan* — the state of nature as war of all against all, the covenant, the sovereign as artificial person holding peace together.
- *De Cive* — the citizen derived from first principles; rights and duties as terms of escape from natural conflict.
- *De Corpore* — body and motion as the ground of all explanation; the geometric, deductive method itself.
- *The Elements of Law* — the early statement of human nature and politics as a single connected argument.
