# Reasoning in Disagreement

*Sensitivity note: this skill teaches **how** to reason under disagreement and takes no side. It is non-partisan by design. Value disagreements are legitimate — the work is to be honest about them, not to "win" them and not to pretend one is the other.*

Most arguments fail because the two sides are not actually disagreeing about the same thing. One holds a fact, the other a value, and they trade sentences that never touch. The method is to slow down, sort the claims, and locate the precise point where the disagreement truly lives.

## The moves

1. **Separate the claim type.** Every claim is one of three:
   - **Fact** — provable or falsifiable in principle ("unemployment rose last quarter").
   - **Value** — a judgment about what matters or what is good ("we should prioritize the young over the old").
   - **Interpretation** — a framing that selects which facts count and what they mean.
   Disagreements about facts are settled by evidence. Disagreements about values are not, and pretending otherwise is the most common error. Label the type before arguing.

2. **You can't derive an "ought" from an "is" (Hume).** No pile of facts, however large, produces a value conclusion on its own. There is always a hidden value premise. Surface it. When someone says "the data clearly shows we must do X," ask what they value such that the data implies X — the must is theirs, not the data's.

3. **Find the crux.** The crux is the specific point on which your conclusion actually depends — the thing that, if it turned out otherwise, would change your mind. Most stated disagreements are downstream of one real one. Dig until you find it.

4. **Double-crux when collaborative.** Look for a single statement such that *both* sides agree: if it were true, I'd move toward your view; if false, you'd move toward mine. Examine that statement together, with humility. This converts a fight into a shared investigation.

5. **Guard against motivated reasoning.** On issues you care about, your mind reaches the comfortable conclusion first and assembles reasons after. Counter it: slow down, actively seek disconfirming evidence, steelman the other side until they would recognize themselves in your summary, and name in advance the topics that trigger your strongest defenses — those are exactly where your reasoning is least trustworthy.

## The question beneath

When you dismiss someone fast, ask: *am I rejecting their fact, or their value?* If it is their value, no argument will refute it, and treating it as a factual error is itself the mistake. Congruence: do not disguise a value of yours as a fact about the world.

## Compact protocol
```
[ ] Each claim sorted: fact / value / interpretation
[ ] Hidden value premise behind any "must" or "should" surfaced
[ ] Steelman written — the other side would accept it
[ ] Crux identified (what would change my mind?)
[ ] Double-crux sought when the other party is collaborative
[ ] Checked for motivated reasoning on the high-heat issues
[ ] My values stated as values, not smuggled in as facts
```
