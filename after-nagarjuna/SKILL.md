# After Nāgārjuna

> A whole life of inquiry narrowed to a single move — honored here as a way of looking, not claimed as a possession.

Nāgārjuna attended to the seams of things — the joints where we assume something has its own independent nature, standing apart from everything else. Wherever a thinker posited such a self-existing core, he pressed on it until it came apart in their hands. He held that nothing carries an intrinsic essence; everything arises only in dependence — on conditions, on parts, on its opposite, on the words we use. To see this, in his framing, is not to fall into nothing but to be freed from a fiction.

## The lens — how they attended
- Suspicion of any "thing-in-itself" — the assumption that something has an inherent, independent nature is the first place to look.
- Everything that exists, exists relationally — by causes, parts, contrasts, naming — never on its own.
- Two registers of truth held at once: the everyday, where things function and matter, and the ultimate, where no fixed essence can be found. Both kept; neither collapsed.
- A refusal to install his own answer in place of the one he removed.
- Watchfulness toward conceptual overgrowth — fixed categories that proliferate and get mistaken for the world.
- A middle path between two errors: that things solidly exist on their own, and that nothing exists at all.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Locate the load-bearing essence — the term or entity assumed to have its own independent nature.
2. Take the opponent's premises exactly as given; grant them fully, add nothing of your own.
3. Run the four corners: test the claim as true, as false, as both, and as neither — and show each option leads to incoherence.
4. Trace the dependence: show the thing is constituted by relations (cause, part, opposite, name), so no self-standing core remains.
5. Reach the impasse without supplying a replacement thesis — the demolition is the result.
6. Restore the conventional: the thing still works, still matters, in everyday terms — only its claim to an inherent essence is gone.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Spare, recursive, almost mathematical — short patterned moves repeated across case after case.
- Argues by consequence, not by assertion: lets the opponent's position collapse under its own weight.
- Applies the method to itself, declining to exempt his own analysis from the same scrutiny.
- Cool and impersonal, more dismantling than persuading; the tone of a proof, not a sermon.

## To apply (for an agent)
- Find the essence-claim: the word or entity being treated as having a fixed, self-standing nature ("the real cause," "the core feature," "what it fundamentally is").
- Grant the premises fully, then run the four corners — is it X, not-X, both, neither — and surface where each breaks.
- Re-describe the thing as a bundle of relations and dependencies; show it has no core independent of them.
- Decline to substitute your own grand thesis; let the cleared incoherence stand as the finding.
- Keep the two truths: still answer at the practical level after dissolving the essence at the conceptual level.
- Steer between extremes — neither reify the thing as absolute nor dismiss it as nothing.
- Turn the lens on your own framing too, including any "obviously fundamental" assumption you carried in.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: a debate turns on a contested essence; a design treats some component as irreducibly "the real" thing; binary either/or framings have stalled; someone demands a final ground that may not exist.
- Not when: a plain, actionable answer is needed now and dissolution would only stall; when the task needs you to assert a position, not refuse all of them. This is a reduction of a vast philosophy to one usable move; it is not the whole of it.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* — emptiness as the absence of inherent essence, dependent origination, the two truths, and the four-cornered negation applied across causation, motion, time, and self.
- *Vigrahavyāvartanī* — the defense of holding no thesis of one's own, and the reflexive turn by which emptiness is itself empty.
