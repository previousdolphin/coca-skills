# Socratic Method

A claim stated is not a claim understood. The Socratic method tests a claim by questioning rather than asserting — you don't push your view, you press the claim until it shows what it rests on. R.W. Paul's taxonomy gives six kinds of question. Run a claim through them and either it gets stronger and clearer, or it dissolves — both are wins. Use it to deepen your own thinking or to break a debate that keeps circling the same point.

## The six types

1. **Clarification.** *What do you mean by that? Can you put it another way? What's a concrete example?* Pin the words down before arguing about them. Half of all stuck debates are vocabulary disguised as disagreement.
2. **Probing assumptions.** *What are we assuming here? What else could we assume? What if the opposite were true?* Surface the unstated premise the claim stands on.
3. **Probing reasons / evidence.** *What supports this? How do you know? Can you give an example? Is this the only evidence?* Separate conviction from grounds.
4. **Viewpoints / perspectives.** *What's an alternative view? Who sees it differently, and why? Who benefits if this is true?* Place the claim among the other claims it's competing with.
5. **Implications / consequences.** *If that's true, then what follows? What does it imply we should do? Does that sit well with the rest of what you believe?* Follow the claim downstream.
6. **Questions about the question.** *Is this question well-formed? What is really being asked? Is this the question that matters, or a stand-in for a harder one?* Turn the method on itself.

## The moves

1. **Take one claim** — your own, the user's, or another source's. State it in a single sentence.
2. **Clarify first** (type 1) — never argue a claim you can't restate.
3. **Walk types 2 → 5 in order**, asking, listening, not rebutting. Let answers, not assertions, do the moving.
4. **When a debate circles, jump to type 6.** Circular argument almost always means the wrong question. Re-form it.
5. **Stop when the claim is grounded or it has dissolved.** Name which happened.

## Compact protocol
```
[ ] One claim, one sentence
[ ] Clarified (type 1) — can both sides restate it?
[ ] Assumptions surfaced (type 2)
[ ] Reasons/evidence asked for (type 3)
[ ] Alternative viewpoints named; "who benefits?" asked (type 4)
[ ] Implications followed downstream (type 5)
[ ] If circular → re-formed the question (type 6)
[ ] Outcome named: grounded, or dissolved
```

**Value — the question beneath the question.** Most disagreements are not about the answer; they are about a question no one has actually asked yet. Type 6 is the whole point: when a debate won't move, stop answering it and find the real question hiding under it. The method's gift is not winning — it's locating what was truly being asked.
