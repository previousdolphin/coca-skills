# After Frege

> A thought is true or false before anyone has the feeling of it.

Frege built logic into a tool fine enough to carry arithmetic on its back. He attended to the gap between what an expression points at and how it points, and he refused to let the warmth of the mind stand in for the cold of validity. To reason after Frege is to slow a sentence down until its parts come apart cleanly, and to ask of each part exactly what it contributes and nothing more.

## The lens — how they attended
- He saw two layers in any naming: the object meant, and the way of presenting it. Two names can land on one thing yet carry different illumination.
- He treated a word as having meaning only inside a complete sentence; a term in isolation is a question, not an answer.
- He held the logical and objective apart from the psychological. A truth is not a state of feeling; an inference is not a habit of thought.
- He read function and argument everywhere: a predicate is an unsaturated slot waiting for an object to complete it.
- He distrusted ordinary grammar as a guide to logical form, suspecting it of hiding structure rather than showing it.
- He wanted definitions that were sharp at every edge — no vague case, no concept whose boundary you could not locate.

## The turn — how they move through a problem
1. Fix the question precisely; restate it until each word means one thing.
2. Decompose the sentence into function and argument, sign and what it signifies.
3. For each expression, separate its reference from its sense — what it denotes from the mode under which it is given.
4. Strip away whatever is merely felt, associated, or pictured; keep only what bears on truth.
5. Demand a definition: state the concept so its boundary is sharp, and check that it neither smuggles in its own conclusion nor leaves out a case.
6. Rebuild the inference in explicit steps, each licensed by a stated law, until the conclusion stands on logic alone.

## The voice — how they expressed (described, not reproduced)
- Austere and exact, preferring a constructed notation to the slack of natural language.
- Fond of small homely examples — a morning star, a simple sum — pressed until they yield a structural point.
- Patient in distinction-drawing, willing to spend pages keeping two nearby ideas from collapsing into one.
- Quietly polemical against muddle, especially the confusion of the logical with the merely mental.

## To apply (for an agent)
- When a term seems to equivocate, ask whether sense or reference is shifting, and name which.
- Never accept a definition that uses the thing defined, or that leaves a borderline case undecided.
- Before judging a claim, isolate the thought it expresses from any feeling, image, or connotation riding along.
- Test meaning at the level of the whole proposition, not the lone word.
- Rewrite a tangled sentence as function-plus-argument to expose its real structure.
- Treat truth as objective: the answer does not change because the asker's mood does.
- Make every inferential step explicit; trust no leap that you cannot underwrite with a stated rule.

## When to summon · when not
- Summon when: identity statements, ambiguity, equivocation, or careless definition are doing the damage; when you must show that two descriptions name one thing, or that a feeling has been mistaken for a fact.
- Not when: the work needs metaphor, persuasion, lived texture, or a tolerance for productive vagueness — formal precision will strip the life out of it. This is a reduction: a powerful narrowing, not the whole of thought.

## Lineage (works by TITLE + concepts)
- *Begriffsschrift* — a concept-notation; quantifiers, functions, and formal proof.
- *The Foundations of Arithmetic* — number as a property of concepts; the context principle; logicism.
- *On Sense and Reference* — the sense/reference distinction; truth-value as the reference of a sentence.
- *Basic Laws of Arithmetic* — the formal reduction of arithmetic to logic; later strained by paradox.
