# Second-Order Thinking

First-order thinking stops at the immediate result. Second-order thinking asks what the immediate result *causes* — and what that causes. Most bad decisions are not first-order mistakes; they are correct first-order moves with unexamined consequences downstream.

This is the **Generational Clause**: decide for the ones downstream. The people who inherit this decision can't vote on it now. Trace far enough that you'd defend the choice to them.

## The moves

1. **Name the first-order effect.** The thing the decision does immediately, on purpose. State it cleanly.
2. **Ask "and then what?" — 2 to 3 levels deep.** Each effect becomes a cause. Follow the chain. Stop when consequences stop being plausible, not when they stop being comfortable.
3. **Look for feedback loops.** Systems adapt to your intervention. Who routes around it? What incentive did you just create? The second-order effect is often the system pushing back.
4. **Run the timeline lens.** Ask the same decision at three horizons: 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years. A choice that's good at all three is rare and worth noticing; a choice good only at 10 minutes is usually a trap.
5. **Match rigor to reversibility.** One-way doors (irreversible) get full second-order scrutiny. Two-way doors (cheap to undo) get a fast pass — over-analyzing reversible decisions is its own waste.

## Protocol

```
DECISION:     what we're about to do
1ST ORDER:    immediate intended effect
AND THEN?:    2nd-order  → 3rd-order  (chain the consequences)
LOOPS:        how the system adapts / who routes around it
TIMELINE:     10 min   /   10 months   /   10 years
DOOR:         one-way (full rigor) | two-way (fast pass — just decide)
```

## When to reach for it

- Any irreversible or expensive-to-undo decision.
- A "fix" that keeps generating new problems — that's unexamined second-order effects arriving on schedule.
- Incentive changes, policy, anything other agents will adapt to.

## Failure modes this prevents

- Solving the visible problem, creating three invisible ones.
- Optimizing the 10-minute horizon against the 10-year one.
- Burning equal scrutiny on reversible and irreversible decisions alike.

Hold the chain loosely past level three — confidence in long-range prediction decays fast. The aim is to see the next move's shadow, not to forecast the future.
